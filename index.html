Return events analyzed

NBA return-to-play: internal load injuries and team-level medical outcomes

Documented returns across 15 seasons (2011–2025), focused on knee, plantar and calf.

Some teams clear injured players to play sooner than the league average, and a chunk of them do it without paying a recurrence price. Others bring players back fast and keep losing them to the same injury. The question I cared about: is that gap real, or is it severity, usage, or noise pretending to be a medical signal?

I split clusters of teams by their fast-return rate, then stress-tested the recurrence gap with severity controls, usage matching, and a within-player view. The 18-percentage-point spread held up under every check that mattered. Even broadening the outcome to all load injuries kept the gap statistically significant, just smaller (8.5pp).

Problem
Do fast-return teams pay a recurrence cost on internal load injuries?
Data
5,000+ return events · 15 seasons · 30 teams (2011–2025).
Methods
Cluster comparison, within-player check, severity & usage matching, broad-outcome sensitivity.
Result
~18pp recurrence spread (38% vs 20%, p < 0.001); ~17pp preserved within-player.

Some teams really are returning players faster and keeping them on the floor. The difference doesn't disappear when you control for who they're treating.

Per-game injury risk in dense windows

Effects of cumulative workload on player injuries

Capstone, Hebrew University. A causal estimate of short-term workload buildup on overuse injury risk.

The naive numbers say players who run more get hurt less. That's because healthier players run more. A textbook selection problem. The interesting question is: conditional on being available to play, does a denser recent schedule actually push overuse injury risk up?

I built injury-opportunity windows from the league's injury logs, joined them to advanced player and game tables, and ran a propensity-score match plus difference-in-differences. To break the remaining selection, I used schedule density as an instrument (ITT), then stress-tested with a falsification on contact injuries and a double-ITT carryover analysis.

The signal didn't blink. Dense exposure raises overuse injury risk by ~25% per game in treated windows, and the contact-injury falsification stays flat, exactly what you want to see if the mechanism is workload, not bad luck.

Problem
Causal effect of cumulative workload on overuse injury risk.
Data
Injury logs joined to advanced game/player tables; rolling 7-day load features.
Methods
PSM, DiD, ITT (schedule density), contact-injury falsification, double-ITT carryover.
Result
~25% per-game risk increase in dense windows; falsification stays flat.

Naive correlations flip once you separate "playing because healthy" from "playing because the schedule forces it." The real signal is the second one, and it's expensive.

Bot flows shipped

Telegram expense tracking bot

A chat-first replacement for the spreadsheet nobody actually opens.

I wanted to log expenses without opening an app. So I built a Telegram bot that takes a one-line message, files it under a category, and answers "where did my money go this month?" with a single command.

Under the hood it runs on Python, switches between SQLite (local) and PostgreSQL (Render) with one env flag, and ships with a Render blueprint so deploying is two clicks. Five live flows: add, list, delete, compare, chart.

Problem
Frictionless expense logging that survives past week one.
Data
User-level transactions by date and category, in SQLite or PostgreSQL.
Methods
Python Telegram bot, inline command UX, env-based DB switch, Render worker blueprint.
Result
Monthly category pie + month-over-month compare in one command.

A small tool that ships, with a deploy story that fits on a sticky note.
