Effects of cumulative workload on player injuries (capstone summary)

Student ID 207677808 · Hebrew University

Causal study on NBA game load and injury risk using observational game- and player-level data (injury logs, advanced box scores). I engineered rolling 7-day workload features (games played, minutes, back-to-back flags, usage intensity) and linked each game appearance to a short post-game injury window to define injury opportunities at scale.

The analysis moves from naive associations to causal estimators: propensity score matching (PSM) to balance high-load vs low-load players on observables, difference-in-differences (DiD) to compare changes in risk around load shifts, and an intention-to-treat (ITT) design that exploits exogenous schedule density (team calendar congestion) rather than coach-discretionary minutes, addressing selection and survivorship bias (including a falsification check on contact injuries). Outcomes were also framed as per-game injury risk to separate “more exposure games” from “riskier minutes.” A follow-up sequence analysis used a double ITT setup to study carryover from the prior week’s congestion when current-week schedule intensity is held fixed.
